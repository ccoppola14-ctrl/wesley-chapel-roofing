Storm Damage to Your Roof? What Wesley Chapel Homeowners Need to Know

Florida storms don't wait for convenient timing. Here's exactly what to do in the hours, days, and weeks after a storm damages your roof — from emergency protection to insurance settlement.

📅 February 2025 ⏱️ 7 min read ✍️ Wesley Chapel Roofing Pro

A severe storm rolls through Wesley Chapel, and by the time it passes, you're staring at ceiling stains, debris in the yard, and sections of shingles scattered across the neighborhood. Your stomach drops. What happens now?

This guide is written for exactly that moment. We'll walk through everything — what to do right now, how to assess the damage safely, how to deal with your insurance company, and how to make sure you're getting everything you're entitled to under your policy.

The First 24 Hours: What to Do Right Now

1. Stay Safe — Don't Get on Your Roof

We know the instinct is to go check the damage immediately. Please don't. Storm-damaged roofs are unstable, wet, and often have compromised structural elements that aren't visible from the outside. Wait for a professional inspection. Every year, homeowners are seriously injured trying to assess their own storm damage.

2. Document Everything From the Ground

While you're waiting, start documenting. Take photos and video of:

  • Any visible damage from the ground (missing shingles, damaged fascia, bent gutters)
  • Interior damage — ceiling stains, wet insulation visible through attic hatch, water intrusion
  • Debris in your yard that came from your roof
  • Any water coming into your home
  • Date and time stamps on everything

This documentation is critical for your insurance claim. The more thorough your initial documentation, the stronger your claim.

3. Protect Your Home from Further Damage

Insurance policies require you to take "reasonable steps to prevent further damage" after a storm. This means:

  • If you have an active leak, place buckets and protect valuables below
  • Move furniture and electronics away from wet areas
  • If a large section of roof is missing, call us for emergency tarping — a proper tarp installation is much better than DIY with a hardware store tarp

4. Call Your Insurance Company to Open a Claim

Don't wait on this. Contact your insurance company or agent to open a claim as soon as possible after the storm. In Florida, insurers are required to acknowledge receipt of your claim within 14 days and make a coverage decision within 90 days — but the process starts when you call. Write down your claim number and the adjuster assigned to your case.

Getting a Professional Storm Damage Assessment

This is the step that separates homeowners who get adequate insurance settlements from those who don't. A thorough professional roof inspection after a storm looks at things that are completely invisible from the ground:

  • Shingle granule loss — wind and hail strip granules from shingles, drastically shortening their life even when shingles look intact from the street
  • Impact marks on shingles and metal components — hail creates distinctive patterns that adjusters are trained to evaluate
  • Lifted, creased, or displaced shingles — wind gets under shingles and breaks the seal strips, allowing water infiltration even when the shingles are still present
  • Flashing damage — storm winds often damage the metal flashing around chimneys, vents, and roof penetrations
  • Gutter and soffit damage — often overlooked but significant damage that should be included in your claim
  • Decking damage — if water has been getting in, the plywood underneath may already be compromised

When we conduct a storm damage inspection, we provide a comprehensive written report with photographs of every identified damage area. This report is specifically formatted to give your insurance adjuster exactly what they need to make a proper coverage determination — and to support supplemental claims if the initial settlement is inadequate.

Understanding the Insurance Claims Process

Step 1: The Adjuster Visit

After you open a claim, your insurance company will send an adjuster to assess the damage. Here's what most homeowners don't realize: insurance adjusters work for the insurance company, not for you. Their assessment determines how much the company will pay — and there's a financial incentive for them to find less damage than actually exists.

We strongly recommend having a roofing professional present during the adjuster's visit. We can point out damage the adjuster might miss, answer technical questions, and ensure the assessment is thorough. This single step has resulted in significantly better outcomes for our customers.

Step 2: The Initial Estimate

After the adjuster visit, you'll receive an estimate from your insurance company showing what they believe the repair or replacement will cost. This initial estimate is often a starting point, not a final settlement.

Compare the insurance estimate to your own contractor's scope of work. Common areas where insurance estimates fall short:

  • Missing damage items the adjuster didn't document
  • Outdated material prices (roofing costs have risen significantly)
  • Omitted code-required upgrades (Florida building code often requires certain improvements when replacing a roof)
  • Missing line items for debris removal, permits, and overhead/profit

Step 3: Supplements and Negotiations

When the insurance estimate doesn't match the actual cost of proper repairs, we submit a supplement — an itemized document explaining the discrepancy and providing documentation for each additional line item. Insurance supplements are extremely common and a normal part of the process.

Don't accept a settlement that doesn't cover the actual cost of restoring your roof to proper condition. Florida law gives you the right to dispute inadequate settlements.

Step 4: Depreciation and ACV vs. RCV

If your policy covers "Actual Cash Value" (ACV), the insurance company will depreciate your roof based on age and condition, paying you what they calculate your damaged roof was worth — not what it costs to replace it. "Replacement Cost Value" (RCV) policies pay the full replacement cost after you complete the repairs. Check your policy — this distinction is enormous.

Choosing the Right Contractor After a Storm

After major storms, Wesley Chapel and Pasco County are flooded with roofing contractors from out of state. Some are legitimate; many are not. Here's how to protect yourself:

  • Verify Florida licensure. Check MyFloridaLicense.com before signing anything. An unlicensed contractor exposes you to enormous liability and voids your manufacturer warranties.
  • Confirm they pull permits. Storm damage repairs in Florida typically require permits. Any contractor who says "we don't need a permit" is either wrong or lying.
  • Research their physical presence. How long have they been in the area? Do they have local references? Can you visit their office? A company with no local roots won't be around for warranty claims.
  • Be wary of "assignment of benefits" arrangements. Signing over your insurance benefits to a contractor gives them control over your claim. Florida law allows this but it often leads to complications.
  • Get everything in writing. Scope of work, materials, warranty terms, payment schedule — all in a signed contract before work begins.

Why You Shouldn't Wait

Every week your damaged roof goes unaddressed:

  • Water infiltration continues to damage decking, insulation, and interior finishes
  • Mold begins to develop in wet insulation (often within 24–48 hours of saturation)
  • Your insurance claim timeline is ticking (Florida requires claims within 3 years, but documentation gets harder over time)
  • Storm chaser contractors are actively soliciting your neighbors — the good local contractors book up fast after major storms

The sooner you act, the better your outcome on every dimension: less additional damage, a stronger insurance position, and more contractor options.
